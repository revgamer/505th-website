HOSPITAL CORPS // MEDICAL DIVISION

HOSPITAL CORPS HANDBOOK

v1.0 — 505th Expeditionary Force Medical Guide

The mission of the UNSC Marine Hospital Corps is to preserve the fighting strength of Marine forces across all theaters of conflict. We provide rapid, skillful, and effective medical care on the battlefield; stabilize and evacuate the wounded. Through unwavering professionalism, advanced medical expertise, and fearless commitment under fire, the Marine Hospital Corps stands as the frontline shield between life and death — saving Marines, sustaining missions, and upholding the highest ideals of service to humanity.

2 BASIC MEDICAL

2.0 Introduction

The goal of basic medical training is to refresh everyone on first aid. You will learn how to assist unconscious troopers during combat using bandages, tourniquets, head turning, hyperextending the head, and recovery position.

Those who complete the steps properly can bring a trooper back up without a medic. Failure can happen due to time, blood loss, or past injuries. A stabilized trooper is a trooper not bleeding with cleared airways. Always call a corpsman when able.

2.1 Basic First Aid Equipment & Actions

Equipment

  • Bandage Elastic
  • Bandage Packing
  • Bandage QuickClot
  • Tourniquets

Actions

  • Airway — Head Turning
  • Airways — Hyperextending Head
  • Chest — Recovery Position
  • Heart Rate Check

2.2 Step by Step Guide to First Aid

A Stabilized Trooper Means:

No bleeding. No blocked airways. Blood loss at "lost some blood" or none. Has a heart rate.

  1. Secure the area. Depending on contact level, engage or provide first aid. Your first priority is making sure the area is clear.
  2. Check trooper status and relay down trooper status to medic and squad leader.
  3. Place tourniquets then bandage wounds until there is no more bleeding. Heavy wounds require tourniquets first. Note: tourniquets block EVERYTHING including auto injectors, IV blood, TXA and EACA.
  4. Check airways. Occlusion → head turning until removed. Obstruction → hyperextend head or place in recovery position and await medic.
  5. Check heart rate. If bleeding stopped, airways clear, and blood loss is minimal, the trooper has the best chance of waking up.

The basics are important. Use what you learn to assist other enlisted and increase the survivability of your squad.

2.3 Final Test

You will provide basic first aid to either: A — Stabilize the patient and allow them to wake up without further aid, or B — Prep the patient for a medic (stabilized = not bleeding).

Failure is missing any vital stabilization steps or the patient dying.

3 ADVANCED MEDICAL

3.0 Introduction

You will learn C.A.C.B — Circulation, Airways, Circulation (Advanced) and Breathing. You must resolve each section before moving to the next. Sometimes you may need to backtrack due to reopening bandages or the patient vomiting.

3.1 Circulation

Associated with bleeding, blood loss, and the heart. At fatal blood loss, the patient loses heart rate and enters cardiac arrest — you have less than 5 minutes.

  1. Stop the bleeding — Use TXA (auto bandage) and EACA (auto stitch) on heavy wounds. These won't work on tourniqueted limbs.
  2. Apply tourniquets to arms and legs to block bleeding. Note: tourniquets block ALL medications on that limb.
  3. Bandage priority: Head → Chest → Least damaged untourniqueted limb.
  4. Insert IV on an open limb (or IO IV) → provide blood 500ML-1000ML. Keep topping up until patient reaches "lost some blood".
  5. Stitch all bandaged wounds (head, chest, untourniqueted limb).

Skipping any of these steps may result in delayed recovery, supply waste, or death.

3.2 Airways

Associated with oxygen and lungs. Always watch blood levels and top off when low.

  1. Confirm circulation is complete.
  2. Check airways — Is there an occlusion? An obstruction?
  3. Occlusion → Use Airway Suction (replaces head turning for medics).
  4. Obstruction → Use King LT. Without it, hyperextend head or use recovery position as alternatives.

3.3 Circulation (Advanced) — Heart Restart

Caused by fatal blood loss, overdose, or heavy impact wounds. Uses AED with heart rate monitor, manual HR check, and Epinephrine.

Patient blood loss must be at "lost some blood" before attempting heart restart.

No AED available?

Treat all cardiac arrest as asystole — CPR until heart rate is restored. Timer to death slows by 50% during CPR.

  1. Patient must be at "lost a lot of blood" for accurate readings, "lost some blood" for best survival chance.
  2. Apply AED pads to patient.
  3. Check HR manually AND via AED monitor.
  4. Confirm rhythm type → apply treatment (CPR or Shock).
  5. Continue until shockable rhythm achieved or HR restored.

3.4 Breathing

Associated with chest and lungs. Two conditions affect breathing:

Pneumothorax

Air trapped between lungs and chest. Short breaths. Treatment: Chest Seal → Needle Decompression → Auscultate.

Hemothorax

Blood trapped between lungs and chest. Gurgling/liquid sounds. Treatment: Chest Seal → Drain Fluids → Auscultate.

Both will result in oxygen loss and eventually death if untreated. Use the Auscultate action to diagnose which condition the patient has. Deep penetrating wounds are a strong indicator.

3.5 Stabilized / Wakeup

Wakeup Criteria:

Blood loss = Lost some blood. No open wounds. Heart rate above 40 BPM. Blood pressure above 60. Oxygen saturation above 85%.

A stabilized person may wake up on their own. Tools to increase wakeup chances: Reorientate action, Carbonate oral pill, Epinephrine auto injector.

3.6 Final Test

You will be provided a severely injured trooper who has reached large blood loss or cardiac arrest. Bring them back using everything you've learned. No assistance from third parties or the instructor.

Pass:

Trooper survives.

Fail:

Trooper dies. 2 attempts total.
